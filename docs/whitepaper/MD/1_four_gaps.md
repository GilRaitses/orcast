# 1. The four gaps in encounter forecasting

Wildlife encounter forecasting produces maps of predicted animal presence or detection probability. In practice these products present four compounding failures.

The **epistemic gap** is that a map shows an intensity or probability without exposing what evidence earned it. Users see a hotspot but cannot determine whether it reflects genuine behavioral ecology or an artifact of where observers look. Users of AI-assisted spatial forecasts systematically overtrust displayed confidence when the basis of that confidence is not made visible alongside the prediction \cite{arxiv200102114}. Survey practitioners have documented that uncertainty is routinely suppressed in data-driven maps even when authors know it exists \cite{arxiv190801697}. The problem is not that maps are untrustworthy; it is that the contract between evidence quality and displayed certainty is not explicit.

The **effort gap** is that sightings and acoustic detections conflate animal presence with observer presence. Movement ecology models of encounter rates show that the classical mass-action approximation fails whenever animals have home ranges smaller than the observation area or when observer search effort is spatially structured \cite{arxiv190705902}. In cetacean monitoring, shore observers concentrate near access points, ferries, and interpretable coastlines, which means that naive hotspot aggregation learns where people watch, not where whales travel. The machine learning wildlife conservation literature acknowledges this as a central unresolved problem in field data pipelines \cite{arxiv211012951}.

The **provenance gap** is that no existing operational system exposes a per-prediction drilldown from a displayed probability to the kernels, detections, and gate verdicts that produced it. A researcher cannot audit whether a displayed confidence of 62 percent reflects a fitted diel kernel that passed a phase-shuffled null test and a held-out deviance skill check, or a prior-dominated estimate with no model fit. Static methodology documents and AWS console queries are not field-accessible provenance.

The **governance gap** is that citizen-science shore reports and automated model promotions enter operational forecasting narratives without explicit quarantine or signed human authority. The information quality literature on citizen science establishes that unmoderated crowdsourced data produces systematic quality failures when used directly in probabilistic models without validation pipelines \cite{arxiv190508289}. The Gravity Spy program at LIGO provides a concrete operational precedent: citizen classifier outputs are quarantined and validated before influencing detector characterization \cite{arxiv161104596}. Without equivalent governance, bad data and overconfident automation compound silently.

Existing tools address pieces of this. Acoustic review software handles detector QC; static methodology documents exist; spreadsheet-based moderation is common. No operational system combines live forecast, gate battery, per-cell provenance, quarantined community ingest, and immutable promotion audit in one loop backed by a primary transactional database.
