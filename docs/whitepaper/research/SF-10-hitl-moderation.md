## Family summary

The citizen science data quality corpus is strong. The "Citizen Science: An Information Quality Research Frontier" paper (1905.08289) is the key theoretical paper: it frames citizen science as a user-generated content problem and establishes that data quality management — including validation, expert review, and quarantine of uncertain contributions — is a necessary condition for scientific usability. The Gravity Spy paper (1611.04596) is an exemplary case study: LIGO integrates citizen science with machine learning for glitch classification, using a structured workflow in which citizen classifications are aggregated and validated before influencing detector characterization — direct precedent for orcast's moderation queue. The Milky Way Project paper (1406.2692) demonstrates that citizen scientist labels, when used directly as training data without quality control, require ensemble aggregation and calibration against expert ground truth — unmoderated labels alone produce systematic errors. The galaxy morphology paper (1409.7935) documents that hybrid human-ML pipelines outperform either alone, but only when the citizen science data is filtered for quality. The "Science Learning via Participation" paper (1601.05973) establishes that citizen scientists vary widely in reliability, reinforcing the case for individual-level review authority. Across the corpus, the pattern is clear: unmoderated citizen data requires explicit quality control before entering a probabilistic model, and human review authority is the mechanism that provides the guarantee.

**Verdict: Supported** — human review authority before citizen data enters a model is well-established as necessary. Primary citations: 1905.08289 (citizen science IQ frontier), 1611.04596 (Gravity Spy: citizen + ML with structured validation), 1406.2692 (Milky Way Project: label quality control).
