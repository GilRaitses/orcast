## Family summary

The search returns strong methodological literature on effort bias in spatial prediction, though most of the directly relevant papers address wildlife crime hotspots (PAWS, poaching prediction) rather than cetacean encounter rate specifically. The PAWS and related papers (2019, 1805.05356) document that naïve hotspot maps are routinely distorted by patrol effort and observation density — a pattern directly analogous to orca sighting density confounded by kayaker and hydrophone coverage. The encounter-rate paper (1907.05902) formally demonstrates that movement-based encounter rates deviate substantially from mass-action assumptions when home ranges and perception ranges differ, supporting the claim that hotspot aggregation without effort correction reflects observer distribution rather than animal density. The biodiversity-ML survey (2110.12951) confirms that sensor coverage gaps and effort bias are recognized, widespread problems in field data pipelines for conservation. The cetacean translation initiative paper (2104.08614) contextualizes how sparse, effort-biased acoustic data limits inference, consistent with orcast's integrity condition "effort assumed continuous." The search did not surface an ecoacoustics or cetacean-specific paper that numerically quantifies the magnitude of effort-bias overestimation for orca hotspots — that gap is an honest limit of the current corpus, and the whitepaper should cite the methodological analogs while acknowledging the absence of a direct cetacean measurement.

**Verdict: Partial** — methodological support is strong; a cetacean-specific effort-correction number is not present in the corpus. Strengthen by citing occupancy-model literature from SDM searches (SF-3, SF-6) and note the open measurement gap as an integrity condition.
